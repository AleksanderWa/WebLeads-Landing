## Who Needs Mortgage Leads

This guide is for people selling services *to* mortgage professionals. Not for homeowners shopping for rates.

That includes:

- **Mortgage software vendors** (CRM, origination systems, compliance tools, digital disclosure)
- **Mortgage lead generation services** (selling leads or lead management platforms to loan officers)
- **Lending technology platforms** (e-signature, document management, underwriting automation)
- **Marketing and recruiting agencies** specializing in mortgage and real estate
- **Third-party service providers** (appraisal management, title services, insurance, notary)
- **B2B services** (coaching, sales training, business development, regulatory compliance)
- **Loan officers and brokers** building their own referral networks and partnerships

All of these need access to active mortgage professionals who are making buying decisions right now.

## Why Mortgage Lists Don't Work

Mortgage is one of the fastest-moving industries. People move between companies constantly. Loan officers switch from banks to brokers to brokerages. Brokers merge, fail, or get acquired. A "top loan officers in California" list bought today is already stale by the time it arrives.

A mortgage software vendor in San Diego wanted to pitch her origination platform to loan officers in Southern California. She bought a "100 active mortgage loan officers" list from a data broker for $500. The names, emails, and phone numbers looked clean on arrival. But when she started outreach, 40% of emails bounced immediately. The addresses no longer existed. Another 30% of phone numbers went to people who said "I don't work here anymore." She ended up with maybe 25 real conversations from a list of 100. By that point, three months had passed since she bought it. She realized the list had been built at least two months before she bought it. And it was resold to dozens of other vendors simultaneously.

The problem is simple: mortgage professionals have high churn and frequent role changes. A list 2-3 months old is not just outdated, it is actively toxic to your outreach metrics. You are paying for stale data and burning through email reputation.

Here is what works: Google Maps is where mortgage companies actively manage their presence. Right now.

It is where customers leave reviews about deal speed and service quality. It is where they post hours, contact info, and the loan programs they offer. It is where they demonstrate they are actively operating this month, not last year.

Instead of buying a list that aged in transit, search Google Maps directly. Here is the full workflow:

## Step 1: Define Your Target Segment

Mortgage is broad. You could target:

- **Retail loan officers** at banks, credit unions, or direct lenders
- **Independent mortgage brokers** (one to two founders, work with multiple lenders)
- **Larger mortgage brokerages** (5-15+ loan officers, multiple branches)
- **Commercial mortgage specialists** (business and investment property financing)
- **Mortgage wholesale lenders** (fund loans, don't originate directly to consumers)

Different segments have different needs and buying power. A solo loan officer building a side practice has a different pain point than a 20-person brokerage running ads across three counties. Target the right one.

## Step 2: Search Google Maps by Location and Business Type

Search for:
- "Mortgage lenders near [city]"
- "Mortgage brokers [metro area]"
- "Loan officers [neighborhood]"
- "FHA loan specialists [county]" (if you want to narrow by product)

You will get every active mortgage company in that location. The results are live. The contact info updates when the business updates it.

## Step 3: Filter by Real Activity Signals

Not all mortgage shops are equally active or professionally run. Filter by signals that matter:

### Review count and recency = deal velocity

Mortgage loans close in 30 to 60 days. When a company closes a loan, the buyer often leaves a review. A mortgage broker with 80 reviews in the last 90 days is actively originating. A broker with 12 reviews from three years ago is not.

Look for:
- **Review count over 40** = established operation with real volume
- **Recent reviews** (last 30-60 days) = currently closing deals, has cash flow
- **Consistent review pace** = predictable deal flow, not a random spike

A broker with five new reviews this month is closing deals this month. Someone with zero reviews in six months is not.

### Google rating and tone

A 4.5+ rating is standard for professional mortgage shops. Below 4.0 often signals customer complaints or slow closings. Read a few recent reviews. What are customers saying? Are they mentioning speed, clarity, or professionalism, or complaining about unresponsiveness and surprises?

### Website quality and product focus

Click the website link on the Google Maps listing. What you see tells you about the company's sophistication and buyer type:

- Do they list specific loan products? (FHA, VA, jumbo, portfolio, non-prime)
- Does the site mention team size or bios?
- Are they actively marketing a niche? (first-time homebuyers, self-employed, real estate investors)
- Is the site modern or 2005?

A broker advertising "FHA loans and first-time buyer programs" is chasing volume and lower-ticket deals. A broker marketing jumbo mortgages and portfolio loans is targeting high net worth or real estate investors and will have a higher deal size and customer sophistication.

### Team size from the Maps listing or website

This matters for deciding who to pitch:

- **Solo operator or two people**: Pitch the owner directly. They are the decision maker.
- **5-15 loan officers**: Find the branch manager or operations lead. They hire tools, set culture, and approve spending.
- **20+ employees**: You need the VP of sales or business development. The owner is three layers away from purchasing decisions.

Google Maps often shows team members or mentions "team of X loan officers" in the description. If not, visit the website or call to confirm size before you build your list.

## Step 4: Understand Retail vs. Wholesale vs. Correspondent

These are different business models with different decision makers and sales cycles:

**Retail** = The company originates loans and often services them. They have direct consumer relationships, marketing spend, and a sales team. Decision maker: owner, branch manager, or VP of sales.

**Wholesale** = The company works through mortgage brokers and doesn't touch consumers directly. They are fast-moving and lean. Decision maker: wholesale manager or owner.

**Correspondent** = The company originates loans and immediately sells them to a larger lender. Thin margins, operational focus. Decision maker: ops manager or owner.

Your pitch needs to match their model. A compliance tool has a different value for a 30-person retail operation than for a five-person wholesale shop. Understand which model you are calling on before you dial.

## Step 5: Get Contact Information and Research Depth

For each mortgage professional you target:

- **Website**: See their loan products, team bios, and any marketing claims about speed or specialties
- **Phone**: In Google Maps, right there. Use it for a quick qualification call if you need confirmation of team size or decision maker
- **Email**: Find via their website, LinkedIn, or the Maps listing itself. If the site has a contact form but no direct email, use a tool to discover the owner or manager email
- **LinkedIn**: Check bios. Are the loan officers established or new? Do they have customer testimonials? Is there a sales manager listed?
- **Recent reviews**: Read the last five to ten reviews. What are customers saying? Speed? Clarity? Problems? This tells you whether the company is thriving or struggling

This approach gives you qualified mortgage professionals, not a bulk dump of names.

## From a Maps Card to a First Touch That Doesn't Sound Robotic

Start with one observable fact you can mention honestly. It might be a recent review mentioning speed, a second location the company opened, or a loan product specialization visible on their website.

Your first message should prove you did basic homework, not that you are clever. One sentence of context, one sentence on why you are reaching out, one question that respects their time.

Examples:
- "I saw your team closed three VA loans last month (great reviews). Are you looking to accelerate your VA pipeline?"
- "You guys just opened a second office in Scottsdale. Growth like that usually means you're evaluating new tools. What's your process right now for [your product category]?"
- "Your site mentions FHA and portfolio programs. How do you currently handle compliance documentation across both?"

Keep it direct. Mortgage professionals are busy and do not have patience for generic templates.
